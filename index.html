About

I am an Assistant Professor at emlyon business school, based in Paris, investigating human-technology interaction from a psychological perspective. My work probes how individuals evaluate the promises and risks of generative AI, how they share responsibility with intelligent systems, and which design cues build lasting trust. I am motivated by helping organizations build technology that genuinely benefits people.

After earning my Ph.D. from RWTH Aachen University, I spent a year as a postdoctoral researcher at IÉSEG School of Management. Today, I contribute to emlyon's Marketing, Technology and Customer Insights (MTCI) and Lifestyle research centers, advancing theory and practice around responsible technology deployment.

I also co-organize the EMAC Consumer Behavior Special Interest Group, curating monthly online seminars and conference sessions that spotlight emerging research in consumer behavior.

Living in Paris while teaching across Europe lets me bridge academic insight with the realities of globally distributed, culturally diverse teams experimenting with GenAI-powered tools.

When I am not in the classroom or doing field research, you will likely find me running along the Seine—feel free to connect with me on Strava.

Research Focus

Generative AI adoption

Mapping the psychological and ethical drivers that shape when people embrace or resist generative AI across classrooms, workplaces, and civic life.

Human-robot collaboration

Investigating how frontline employees and consumers attribute responsibility and value when autonomous service technologies join the customer experience.

Technology acceptance gaps

Revealing perception differences between managers and consumers to design interventions that align expectations and increase adoption success.

Prosocial technology design

Examining how prosocial framing, transparency, and human-in-the-loop controls shape disclosure, compliance, and long-term value for AI-enhanced services.

Human-Centered AI & Consumer Expertise

I operate at the intersection of consumer-behavior scholarship and hands-on development—showing how generative AI augments teaching, how human-AI complementarity shifts decision making, and how consumers respond to intelligent services across channels.

Human-centered AI research

Investigating how human values, fairness, and transparency can be embedded into AI systems so that people remain empowered collaborators alongside intelligent tools.

Generative AI for teaching

Designing GenAI classroom interventions—from simulations to thesis co-pilots—that cultivate critical thinking while keeping academic integrity at the forefront.

Human-AI complementarity

Mapping when algorithms should advise, co-create, or step back so that employees and consumers achieve better outcomes together with AI.

GenAI consumer behavior signals

Analyzing how exposure to generative AI reshapes trust, adoption thresholds, and the perceived value of human vs. automated expertise in marketplaces.

GenAI-powered market research

Building interview copilots, qualitative coding pipelines, and insight dashboards that let marketing teams run rapid, human-guided, AI-assisted research sprints from discovery to storytelling.

GenAI Solutions for Organizations

I partner with marketing, insight, and learning teams to prototype and deploy human-centered generative AI solutions—from interview copilots to market sensing dashboards—grounded in rigorous research design.

Selected Publications

A selection of peer-reviewed articles and working papers exploring ethical technology adoption, human-machine collaboration, and consumer perception. Full publication list is available on Google Scholar.

2025

Student (Mis)Use of Generative AI Tools for University-Related Tasks

International Journal of Human-Computer Interaction · DOI: 10.1080/10447318.2025.2462083

Leonhard Reiter, Moritz Joerling, Christoph Fuchs, Robert Boehm

Extends the Technology Acceptance Model to quantify drivers of responsible AI use in higher education and estimate cheating prevalence in exams.

2024

A matter of perspective: Technology contributions are assessed differently from manager and consumer perspectives

Recherche et Applications en Marketing · DOI: 10.1177/20515707241283230

Amanda P. Yamim, Robert P. Mai, Moritz Joerling

Demonstrates how efficiency framing closes the perception gap between managers and consumers when evaluating technology-led innovation.

2023

People devalue generative AI’s competence but not its advice in addressing societal and personal challenges

Communications Psychology · DOI: 10.1038/s44271-023-00032-x

Robert Boehm, Moritz Joerling, Leonhard Reiter, Christoph Fuchs

Shows that awareness of AI authorship reduces perceived competence without diminishing the willingness to follow AI advice, highlighting nuanced AI aversion.

2023

To disclose or not to disclose?: Factors related to the willingness to disclose information to a COVID-19 tracing app

Information, Communication & Society · DOI: 10.1080/1369118X.2022.2050418

Moritz Joerling, Sarah Eitze, Philipp Schmid, Cornelia Betsch, Jennifer Allen, Robert Boehm

Identifies prosocial benefit framing, institutional trust, and perceived ease of use as levers that increase disclosure in tracing apps.

2023

Technoism: A New Form of Prejudice and Discrimination

Working paper

Moritz Joerling, Nico Heuvinck, Gwarlann de Kerviler, Derek D. Rucker

Introduces the concept of technoism—bias against technology based on perceived otherness—and documents its societal impact across twelve studies.

2022

Journal of Service Management · DOI: 10.1108/JOSM-11-2020-0406

Stefanie Paluch, Sven Tuzovic, Heiko Holz, Alexander Kies, Moritz Joerling

Unpacks employee personas and appraisal processes that drive willingness to collaborate with service robots in frontline roles.

Moritz Joerling, Robert Boehm, Stefanie Paluch
